Chapter Five · Types of MOSFET

The Four Types — Built from First Principles

5.0

The MOSFET Family Tree

Every MOSFET belongs to exactly one of four types. These come from two independent manufacturing choices:

Choice 1 — Channel Type

N-Channel: Electrons carry the current. Fast. Built on P-substrate.

P-Channel: Holes carry the current. Slower. Built on N-substrate.

Choice 2 — Mode of Operation

Enhancement (E): No channel at rest. Must CREATE it with gate voltage. Normally OFF.

Depletion (D): Channel pre-built during manufacturing. Must DESTROY it. Normally ON.

The 4 MOSFET Types — Complete Family
💡 Study Strategy

Master N-Channel Enhancement first — it's 90% of what you'll ever use. Once you understand it deeply, the other three types follow by analogy: flip the polarity for P-channel, add a pre-built channel for Depletion.

Part A · Enhancement Mode

E-MOSFET — The Normally OFF Transistor

5.1

What is Enhancement Mode? — The Big Idea

The word "Enhancement" tells you everything. There is no channel present at rest. When you apply a gate voltage, you enhance — you create — a conducting channel out of nothing. Before voltage: no current. After voltage: current flows.

🏗️ Analogy — Building a Bridge

Imagine a river (the P-substrate) separating two towns (the N+ Drain and N+ Source). No bridge connects them — no one can cross. When you apply gate voltage, you are building a temporary bridge (the inversion channel) across the river. Remove the voltage, the bridge disappears. The E-MOSFET constructs its own path on demand.

5.2

N-Channel Enhancement MOSFET (NMOS)

This is the most important and most used MOSFET type. Every processor, memory chip, and digital logic circuit is built predominantly from these.

Construction

N-Channel E-MOSFET — Physical Cross-Section

❓ Why N+ (Heavily Doped) Instead of Just N?

This is a question most textbooks skip. The "+" symbol means heavily doped — far more dopant atoms than a regular N-type region. Here is exactly why this matters:

💡 Why Heavy Doping (N+) is Essential

Think about what we need from the Drain and Source regions:

IF WE USED LIGHTLY DOPED N
  • Few free electrons available
  • High resistance at contacts
  • Voltage drop at metal–silicon junction
  • Rectifying (diode-like) contact → bad!
  • Poor current delivery into channel
WITH HEAVILY DOPED N+
  • Huge electron concentration
  • Very low resistance (near zero)
  • Ohmic contact with metal wire
  • No voltage drop at contact
  • Excellent electron reservoir for channel

The N+ regions act as electron reservoirs. When the channel forms, electrons flood in from the N+ Source — but only if the Source has an abundance of them. Light doping would starve the channel. Heavy doping ensures the channel is well-supplied. Also, the metal wire connecting to Drain/Source makes an ohmic (non-rectifying) contact only when the semiconductor is heavily doped — otherwise you get an unwanted diode junction at every terminal.

❓ Is Drain Always at Higher Voltage (VDD) and Source Always at GND?

This is a very common source of confusion. The short answer: not always by definition, but almost always in practice.

💡 The Real Definition of Source vs Drain

The names come from the direction of conventional current flow:

  • Source = where the current-carrying carriers originate from. In NMOS, electrons come from Source → it's the electron source. Connected to GND (0V) so electrons can flow from it into the channel.
  • Drain = where the carriers are drained away to. In NMOS, electrons arrive here from the channel and flow out. Connected to VDD (positive) to attract the electrons.
  • So yes — in NMOS circuits, Drain is almost always at the higher voltage (VDD side) and Source is at the lower voltage (GND side). This is because electrons (negative charges) flow from low potential to high potential.
  • In PMOS circuits, it is reversed — Source is at VDD and Drain is at the lower voltage, because holes (positive charges) flow from high to low potential.
  • Exception: In some analog circuits (e.g., transmission gates), the MOSFET can be "bidirectional" and Drain/Source can swap roles depending on which terminal happens to be at higher potential during operation.

Layer-by-Layer Construction Explained

1
P-type Silicon Substrate — The Foundation

Pure silicon doped with Boron. Has excess holes (⊕) as majority carriers. The P-type body separates the two N+ regions. At rest, the N+–P–N+ structure forms two back-to-back PN junctions that block any current flow.

2
N+ Drain and N+ Source — Heavily Doped Electron Reservoirs

Two heavily doped N-type islands diffused into the P-substrate. They are electron-rich reservoirs. The gap between them across the P-substrate surface is where the channel will form. Without the channel, these two N+ regions are disconnected — separated by the P-type body creating two back-to-back diodes.

3
Gate Oxide (SiO₂) — The Insulating Magic Layer

An extremely thin layer of silicon dioxide (glass) grown thermally on the silicon surface above the channel region. It is only 5–10 nanometers thick — about 40 silicon atoms wide. This layer is a perfect DC insulator, so no gate current ever flows. But an electric field passes right through it. This is what makes MOSFET draw near-zero gate current.

4
Gate Electrode — The Control Terminal

A conductive layer (polysilicon or metal) deposited on top of the oxide. Applying voltage here creates a vertical electric field that penetrates through the oxide into the silicon below. This field controls whether a channel forms or not.

❓ How Does Applying Voltage to the Gate Create an Electric Field?

💡 The Physics of Electric Field Creation

When you connect a battery between Gate and Source:

  • Positive charges (holes) accumulate on the top surface of the Gate metal (since it's connected to the positive terminal of the battery).
  • These positive charges on the gate create an electric field pointing downward — from the Gate metal, through the oxide, into the P-silicon below. The field lines point from positive to negative (from Gate toward the grounded body).
  • The SiO₂ oxide layer does not allow any current to flow — but it does not block electric fields. The field passes right through the glass like light through a window.
  • Inside the P-silicon, this downward-pointing field exerts forces: it pushes holes (⊕) away (same direction as field = force on positive charges downward, away from surface) and pulls electrons (⊖) upward toward the surface (opposite to field direction = force on negative charges upward).
Electric Field Through Gate Oxide — How It Controls the Channel

Working — How the Channel is Created

Let's trace exactly what happens as we increase Vgs from zero.

Three Stages of Gate Voltage — OFF → Depletion → ON
①
Vgs = 0 — Device OFF (Two reverse-biased PN junctions)

The P-substrate surface between Drain and Source is full of holes. The N+–P junction at the Drain side and P–N+ junction at the Source side form two PN junctions facing opposite directions — like two diodes back-to-back. No matter what Vds you apply, one of those junctions is always reverse-biased and blocks current. Id = 0.

②
0 < Vgs < Vth — Depletion Stage (Field repels holes)

The positive Gate voltage creates a downward electric field. This field repels the positive holes in the P-substrate away from the surface. A depletion region forms — a zone stripped of mobile charges. But not enough electrons have yet been attracted to form a conducting path. Current still cannot flow.

③
Vgs ≥ Vth — Inversion! Channel Forms!

Now the electric field is strong enough to attract minority electrons from the P-substrate (and from the adjacent N+ Source region). These electrons accumulate in a thin layer just below the oxide, effectively converting the surface to N-type behavior. This is the inversion layer — it IS the channel. Drain and Source are now linked by N-type material → current flows.

❓ Why Do Electrons Flow from Source to Drain (Not the Other Way)?

💡 The Driving Force — Electric Potential

Once the channel forms, there are free electrons in it. But why do they move from Source to Drain? There are two forces at work:

  • Electric potential difference (Vds): The Drain is connected to a positive voltage (VDD) and Source is at 0V (GND). This creates an electric field along the channel, pointing from Drain to Source (from high potential to low). Electrons have negative charge, so the force on them is opposite to the field direction — they are pulled toward the Drain (from Source → Drain).
  • Intuitive version: The Drain has positive voltage. Electrons are negatively charged. Opposite charges attract. So electrons in the channel are attracted toward the positive Drain terminal. They flow from Source → through channel → into Drain → out into the circuit.
  • Conventional current direction: By convention, current direction is opposite to electron flow. So conventional current flows from Drain → Source (D to S), even though electrons physically move Source → Drain.
Electron Flow vs Conventional Current Direction in the Channel

❓ Why Is the Channel Width NOT Uniform Along Its Length?

This is a concept most textbooks mention but never truly explain. Let's build the complete physical picture step by step.

💡 The Key Insight — Effective Gate Voltage Varies Along the Channel

The channel thickness at any point depends on the local electric field from the Gate. And that field depends on the voltage difference between the Gate and that exact point in the channel.

The channel is not a fixed conductor — it is a region that is induced by the gate field. The stronger the field at a point, the more electrons are attracted there, the thicker the channel. The weaker the field, the fewer electrons, the thinner the channel.

Now here is the critical physics: the voltage in the channel itself increases from Source to Drain. At the Source end, channel voltage ≈ 0V. At the Drain end, channel voltage ≈ Vds. The effective gate-to-channel voltage at any point along the channel is:

Effective Gate-to-Channel Voltage at any point x along channel
Veff(x) = Vgs − V(x)
Where V(x) = voltage at point x in the channel.
At Source end: V(x) = 0 → Veff = Vgs (maximum → thickest channel)
At Drain end: V(x) = Vds → Veff = Vgs − Vds (minimum → thinnest channel)
Channel Width vs Position — Linear Region vs Saturation

❓ The Pinch-Off Phenomenon — Fully Explained Step by Step

This is the concept that separates students who truly understand MOSFETs from those who just memorize. Let's walk through it carefully.

💡 Step-by-Step: How Increasing Vds Leads to Pinch-Off
①
Start: Vgs fixed above Vth. Vds = 0.

Both Drain and Source are at 0V. No voltage difference along channel. Effective gate voltage is uniform everywhere = Vgs. Channel is uniform in thickness. No current yet (no Vds to drive it).

②
Increase Vds slightly (Linear Region)

Now the Drain is at a positive voltage. Voltage in the channel rises from 0 at Source to Vds at Drain. The effective gate-to-channel voltage at the Drain end becomes Vgs − Vds (less than at Source). The channel is slightly thinner at the Drain end. But overall, channel is still intact — current increases linearly with Vds. This is the Linear (Ohmic) Region.

③
Keep increasing Vds — Channel tapers more

As Vds increases more, the effective gate voltage at the Drain end (= Vgs − Vds) keeps falling. The channel gets progressively thinner at the Drain end. Current still increases, but more slowly now because the channel is thinning.

④
Vds = Vgs − Vth → Pinch-Off Point!

At this exact moment: effective gate voltage at Drain end = Vgs − Vds = Vgs − (Vgs − Vth) = Vth. At Vth, the channel just barely disappears. The channel has been reduced to zero thickness at the Drain end. This is called pinch-off. The channel is NOT gone everywhere — it's still thick at the Source end. It just tapers to zero at the Drain end.

⑤
Vds > Vgs − Vth → Saturation (But why doesn't current stop?!)

Here's the part that surprises everyone. The channel pinches off at the Drain end — but current does NOT stop! Why? Because the pinch-off point is not a wall — it's the edge of a depletion region. Electrons that reach the pinch-off point are swept across this depletion region by the strong electric field there (the field is very strong because Drain has high voltage pulling electrons in). Think of it like a waterfall — the river narrows at the edge, but water doesn't stop; it accelerates over the edge. The rate of current is now determined only by how fast the channel can supply electrons — which depends only on Vgs − Vth (channel strength). Since Vgs is fixed, current is fixed. That's why Id becomes constant in saturation — independent of Vds.

Pinch-Off — The Complete Picture at Vds = Vgs − Vth
🌊 The Waterfall Analogy — Why Current Doesn't Stop at Pinch-Off

Imagine the channel as a river flowing from a lake (Source) toward a cliff (Drain). As Vds increases, the cliff gets higher, and the river narrows near the edge. When the river reaches "pinch-off width" at the cliff edge, the water doesn't stop — it accelerates off the cliff. The amount of water flowing over depends on how much the lake (Source) can supply — which depends on the channel width (set by Vgs). Increasing the cliff height (Vds) doesn't increase flow once the river is at the edge — the rate is limited by the lake's supply rate alone. That's why current saturates.

Working — Operation (Complete Voltage Analysis)

Now let's go through the full operational analysis — with both Vgs and Vds applied simultaneously — exactly as covered in Boylestad's textbook.

N-Channel E-MOSFET — Complete Operating Circuit (Boylestad FIG 6.33 style)
🔑 Operation — Key Points (as in Boylestad's Textbook)
  • When Vgs = 0V and Vds > 0: Absence of an n-channel results in Id = 0A. There are two reverse-biased p–n junctions between Drain and Source that oppose any current flow.
  • When both Vgs and Vds are set positive: The positive gate potential pressures the holes in the p-substrate away from the oxide surface and attracts electrons toward the gate. The SiO₂ layer prevents these electrons from being absorbed at the gate terminal — they accumulate at the surface.
  • As Vgs increases in magnitude: The concentration of electrons near the SiO₂ surface increases until the induced n-type region can support measurable current flow between Drain and Source.
  • The level of Vgs that results in significant increase in drain current is called the threshold voltage (VT).
  • Since the channel is nonexistent at Vgs = 0V and is "enhanced" by positive Vgs, this device is called an enhancement-type MOSFET. Note: Gate current IG = 0A always — the SiO₂ insulates the gate completely.
  • As Vgs increases beyond VT, drain current increases, and the device works as a voltage-controlled current source.

Characteristics — Drain & Transfer Curves (Boylestad FIG 6.35 style)

As Vgs is increased beyond the threshold level, the density of free carriers in the induced channel increases, resulting in an increased level of drain current (Id). If Vgs is kept constant and Vds is increased, the drain current will eventually reach a saturation level as occurred for the JFET and depletion-type MOSFET.

The leveling off of Id is due to a pinching-off process — the narrower channel at the Drain end of the induced channel. Applying Kirchhoff's voltage law: VDG = VDS − VGS. If Vgs is held fixed at 8V and Vds is increased from 2V to 5V, the voltage VDG increases from −6V to −3V, and the gate becomes less and less positive with respect to the Drain — reducing the field strength at the Drain end of the channel.

N-Ch E-MOSFET — Drain Characteristics & Transfer Characteristics
E-MOSFET Transfer Equation (Saturation)
ID = K(VGS − VGS(th))²
K can be determined from datasheet using a known operating point:
K = ID(on) / (VGS(on) − VT)²

Where ID(on) and VGS(on) are values given at a specified operating point on the datasheet.
Also: VDS(sat) = VGS − VT — the Vds at which saturation begins for a given Vgs.
💡 Why is the Transfer Curve a Square Law (not linear)?

The channel width is proportional to (Vgs − Vth). Current is proportional to channel width AND the number of carriers per unit length, which is also proportional to (Vgs − Vth). Multiplying these two proportional quantities together gives a (Vgs − Vth)² relationship — a square law. So doubling the overdrive voltage (Vgs − Vth) gives four times the current, not twice. This nonlinearity is fundamental to MOSFET amplifier design.

5.3

P-Channel Enhancement MOSFET (PMOS)

PMOS is the mirror image of NMOS. Everything is reversed: substrate type, carrier type, voltage polarities. Instead of electrons as carriers, PMOS uses holes. Instead of positive gate voltage, it needs negative gate voltage.

🪞 The Mirror Analogy

If you took an NMOS and flipped all doping types (N→P, P→N) and reversed all voltage polarities, you get PMOS. Same physics, same principles — just mirrored. Why N+ instead of N? Same reason — heavy doping gives ohmic contacts and a good hole reservoir. The P+ regions are the hole sources instead of electron sources.

Part B · Depletion Mode

D-MOSFET — The Normally ON Transistor

5.4

Depletion Mode — The Built-In Channel

In Depletion mode, the channel is physically doped into the device during manufacturing — it exists even with Vgs = 0. To turn it OFF, you must apply a gate voltage that depletes (destroys) the channel carriers.

🚪 Analogy — The Normally Open Door

A door that is always open by default — people (current) walk through freely. To close it, you must actively push it shut (apply negative Vgs to repel channel electrons). Stop pushing → springs back open. This is D-MOSFET: effort needed to turn it OFF, not ON.

🔑 How the Built-In Channel is Made

During manufacturing, the channel region between Drain and Source is deliberately doped with N-type impurities (for N-channel). This creates a permanent N-type strip. It conducts at Vgs = 0. The gate oxide is still present — but instead of needing to induce a channel, the voltage now controls the width of this pre-existing one. Apply negative Vgs → electrons are repelled away → channel narrows → current decreases. Apply positive Vgs → more electrons attracted → channel widens → more current.

⚠ Two Common Mistakes

Mistake 1: Confusing mode (Enhancement vs Depletion) with operating region (Saturation vs Linear). Mode = whether a built-in channel exists. Operating region = how the channel behaves under different Vds. An Enhancement MOSFET in saturation is still an Enhancement-mode device.

Mistake 2: Assuming Drain is always at VDD. In PMOS, Source is at VDD and Drain is at the lower voltage. Always identify the carrier type first, then determine which terminal is at higher potential based on which direction the carriers need to flow.
