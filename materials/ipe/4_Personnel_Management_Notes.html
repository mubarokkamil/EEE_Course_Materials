Personnel Management (HRM) — Smart Notes

1. What is Personnel Management / HRM?

Two major definitions exist — know both for exams.

British Institute of Personnel Management defines it as:

"That part of management which is concerned with people at work and with their relationship within an organization."

Simple meaning: PM/HRM is specifically about managing the human side of an organization — not machines, not money, but people.

American Management Association defines HRM as:

"That field of management which has to do with planning, organizing, and controlling various operative functions of procuring, developing, maintaining, and utilizing a workforce."

Key difference in the AMA definition: it adds the word "controlling" and highlights four operative functions — procuring (getting people), developing (training them), maintaining (keeping them satisfied), and utilizing (getting the best work out of them).

Real-World View: Consider how Grameenphone runs its HR department. It doesn't just hire engineers and forget about them. GP trains them on new 5G technologies (developing), ensures competitive salaries and career paths (maintaining), and assigns them to projects where their skills create maximum value (utilizing). That full cycle is what HRM covers.

2. Objectives of Personnel Management / HRM

HRM is not just about hiring and firing. It has multiple clear objectives:

1. Attract, Develop, Motivate, and Retain talented employees. Companies like Walton or Samsung invest heavily in making themselves attractive workplaces — good salaries, career growth, modern labs — because skilled engineers are rare and valuable.

2. Optimum Utilization of Human Resources. Hiring 100 engineers is useless if 40 of them are doing jobs a technician could do. HRM ensures each person is placed where their skills generate maximum output. For example, BPDB assigns Protection Engineers to relay testing work, not civil construction tasks.

3. Encourage Every Employee to Make Their Best Contribution. The goal is not just compliance ("do your job"), but engagement ("give your best"). Google does this through open project systems (20% time for personal innovation), where engineers feel ownership over their work.

4. Build Respect and Trust Between Management and Workers. When linemen at BPDB trust their supervisors, they work safely and report problems early. When they don't trust management, they hide mistakes and accidents happen. Trust is not soft — it directly affects operational safety and efficiency.

5. Increase Efficiency Through Training, Guidance, and Counseling. A fresh EEE graduate who joins Energypac may not know industrial transformer design. The company must train them. This training investment increases efficiency over time.

3. Importance of HRM

The core insight here is simple but powerful:

Knowledge lives inside people, not machines.

A CNC machine doesn't know how to improve the manufacturing process — the engineer operating it does. A substation doesn't know that the relay settings are wrong — the protection engineer does. This means:

  • Low-quality HR → Low-quality output and customer service. If DPDC hires under-qualified meter technicians, smart pre-paid meters get installed incorrectly, causing billing errors and customer complaints.

  • Effective Knowledge Management → Competitive Advantage and Profits. Intel's competitive edge in chip design is not just their equipment — it is the knowledge of thousands of semiconductor engineers. Lose those engineers to TSMC or Samsung, and Intel loses its advantage.

4. Traditional Sources of Competitive Advantage

Before HRM became central, companies competed through four traditional sources:

  • Financial / Economic Capabilities — who has more capital to invest.
  • Product Capabilities — who makes a better product.
  • Technological / Process Capability — who has better machines or production processes.
  • Organizational Capability — who is structured more efficiently.

The problem? All four of these can be copied over time. A competitor can raise funds, copy a product design, buy the same machines, or restructure their org chart. But human capability is much harder to copy — which is why HRM became the new source of sustainable competitive advantage.

5. How HRM Creates Sustainable Competitive Advantage

Three specific ways:

1. Maximize the Value Added by Employees. Don't just have employees — develop them to contribute at their highest level. For example, Tesla runs intensive internal training programs so its engineers continuously push the boundaries of battery and software design, creating value competitors can't match.

2. Acquire Rare Employees. Some skills are genuinely scarce. BUET graduates with power electronics expertise who also understand machine learning are very rare. Companies that identify and hire these people first gain a major edge. This is why Google and Meta set up recruitment offices near top engineering universities worldwide.

3. Develop a Culture That Can't Be Copied. Apple's culture of design obsession, secrecy, and perfectionism is legendary — and impossible to simply copy. Competitors can build labs and hire designers, but they can't manufacture the specific organizational culture Apple has developed over decades. Culture is the ultimate moat.

6. Human Needs in the Workplace — Maslow's Hierarchy

Why Does This Matter in HRM?

Employees don't all want the same thing. A fresh graduate joining BPDB primarily wants a stable salary and job security. A senior engineer at Grameenphone with 15 years' experience may care more about recognition and creative challenges. HR must understand where each employee sits in the need hierarchy to motivate them correctly.

Maslow's Need Hierarchy Theory

Proposed by Abraham Maslow. There are five levels, and the key rule is: lower-level needs must be satisfied before higher-level needs can motivate someone.

Level 1 — Physiological Needs (Salary, Basic Living) The most basic. An engineer who can't pay rent cannot focus on creative design work. In practice: ensuring a fair salary that covers basic living costs is the foundation of any HRM system.

Level 2 — Safety Needs (Job Security, Safe Workplace) After salary, employees need to know their job is stable and their workplace is safe. For EEE engineers: proper arc flash protection gear, safe substation access protocols, and contract security all fall here. At BPDB, linemen who feel physically unsafe during high-voltage work cannot perform well.

Level 3 — Social / Belongingness Needs (Team, Friendship) Humans are social. Engineers who feel like isolated individuals in a company are less productive than those who feel part of a team. Good HRM builds team culture — project groups, mentorship programs, department events. At Samsung's R&D centers, team cohesion is actively cultivated to improve collaborative innovation.

Level 4 — Esteem Needs (Recognition, Respect) Once belonging is satisfied, people want to be recognized for their contribution. A circuit design engineer at Walton who develops a new inverter technology wants that achievement acknowledged — a promotion, a bonus, or even a public mention in a company meeting. Without this, even well-paid employees become disengaged.

Level 5 — Self-Actualization (Creativity, Innovation, Growth) The highest level. This is where engineers want to do work that challenges them, grows their skills, and lets them create something meaningful. Google's research division, or CERN, or the Rooppur Nuclear Plant's core engineering team — these positions attract people driven by self-actualization. They want to work on the hardest, most meaningful problems.

Key Exam Point: Moving from Level 1 to Level 5, the number of employees who are actively seeking that level decreases. Most employees are operating between Levels 1–3. Very few are driven purely by Level 5.

7. Motivation in HRM

What is Motivation?

Motivation is the process of stimulating employees to perform at their best. It is what makes the difference between an engineer who just completes assigned tasks and one who proactively finds and solves problems.

Motivation can be:

  • Intrinsic — The work itself is satisfying. A researcher at Intel who loves chip design is intrinsically motivated; the work is its own reward.
  • Extrinsic — External rewards drive behavior. A bonus, a promotion, a public award.
  • Financial — Salary increments, bonuses, profit sharing.
  • Non-Financial — Job title, recognition, flexible hours, more interesting project assignments.

Why Motivation Matters in HRM:

  • Improves productivity (motivated engineers solve problems faster and better).
  • Reduces turnover (a motivated Walton engineer won't leave for a 5% salary bump elsewhere).
  • Improves teamwork (motivated individuals want the team to succeed).
  • Encourages innovation (people who care about their work look for better solutions, not just adequate ones).

8. Major Motivation Theories

Theory 1 — Maslow's Need Hierarchy

(Already covered above in Section 6.)

Theory 2 — Herzberg's Two-Factor Theory

Proposed by Frederick Herzberg. The big idea is that job satisfaction and job dissatisfaction are not opposites — they are driven by completely different factors.

Factor 1: Hygiene Factors (Prevent Dissatisfaction — but don't create motivation) These are the baseline conditions. If they are absent or bad, employees are dissatisfied and unproductive. But even if they are excellent, they do NOT actively motivate people — they just remove dissatisfaction.

  • Salary
  • Job Security
  • Working Conditions
  • Company Policy
  • Quality of Supervision

Real-World Example: At PGCB, if engineers are paid below market rate (bad hygiene), they are actively dissatisfied and will leave. But if you pay them the market rate (good hygiene), they don't suddenly become super-motivated — they just stop being dissatisfied. Salary alone doesn't make engineers passionate about their work.

Factor 2: Motivators (Actually Create Satisfaction and Motivation) These are the factors that make employees genuinely engaged and high-performing.

  • Achievement (completing a difficult project successfully)
  • Recognition (being publicly praised for good work)
  • Responsibility (being trusted with a critical system or team)
  • Growth (learning new skills, getting promoted)
  • The Work Itself (finding the job interesting and meaningful)

Real-World Example: When Grameenphone gave a senior network engineer the responsibility to lead the 5G pilot rollout in Dhaka, that engineer became highly motivated — not because of salary, but because of the responsibility, challenge, and recognition the role brought.

The Critical Exam Point:

Removing dissatisfaction ≠ creating motivation. You must provide Motivators to actually motivate employees.

A company that only improves hygiene factors (raises salaries, fixes the cafeteria, improves the office) but never gives recognition, growth, or interesting work will have employees who are "not unhappy" but are not motivated either.

Theory 3 — McGregor's Theory X and Theory Y

Douglas McGregor proposed that managers have fundamentally different assumptions about human nature, and these assumptions shape how they manage.

Theory X — Traditional / Authoritarian View:

  • Employees naturally dislike work and will avoid it if possible.
  • They must be controlled, directed, and threatened with punishment to get them to work.
  • Employees avoid responsibility and prefer to be told what to do.
  • Management style: Strict supervision, micromanagement, punishment-based discipline.

Real-World Example: A factory floor supervisor at an old-style Bangladeshi garments factory who stands over workers with a clipboard, punishing anyone who talks or takes an unscheduled break, is operating under Theory X assumptions.

Theory Y — Modern / Participative View:

  • Employees naturally enjoy meaningful work (just like they enjoy play or rest, when the conditions are right).
  • If committed to goals, employees exercise self-direction and self-control without being supervised.
  • They accept and seek responsibility.
  • They are creative and innovative when given the opportunity.
  • Management style: Delegation, trust, collaborative goal-setting, empowerment.

Real-World Example: Google's engineering culture operates almost entirely on Theory Y assumptions. Engineers are given significant autonomy over how they work, trusted to manage their own time, and encouraged to self-direct. The result is that Google consistently produces some of the most innovative technology in the world.

The Key Insight:

Management style influences employee behavior. If you treat employees like Theory X, they often become Theory X workers (lazy, dependent, uninterested). If you create a Theory Y environment, employees often rise to meet those expectations.

Theory 4 — Expectancy Theory (Victor Vroom)

This theory explains motivation using a simple but powerful formula:

Motivation = Expectancy × Instrumentality × Valence

Let's break each component down:

Expectancy — "If I try hard, will I actually perform better?" An engineer believes that studying control systems thoroughly will result in better performance on the project. If they don't believe effort leads to performance (because the tools are bad, or the project is politically decided), expectancy is low and motivation drops.

Instrumentality — "If I perform well, will I actually get rewarded?" The engineer performs well on the project. But does management actually notice and reward that performance? If promotions in the company are based on seniority rather than merit (common in some government organizations like BPDB), instrumentality is low — good performance doesn't actually lead to better rewards, so engineers stop trying to perform better.

Valence — "Do I actually want the reward being offered?" Even if the engineer believes hard work leads to performance and performance leads to rewards — if the reward itself is something they don't value (e.g., a certificate when they wanted a salary raise), motivation is still low.

Real-World Example: An EEE engineer at a private power company works hard (Expectancy: effort → better output). The company has a clear merit-based promotion system (Instrumentality: performance → promotion). And the engineer genuinely wants that promotion for career growth (Valence: high). All three are high → the engineer is highly motivated.

Counter-example: At a government utility where promotions are purely seniority-based, Instrumentality is near zero — no matter how well an engineer performs, promotion is automatic after years of service. So many engineers stop trying to excel.

9. Defense Mechanisms in HRM

When employees face stress, failure, criticism, or conflict at the workplace, they sometimes respond with psychological defense mechanisms — unconscious ways of protecting their ego from painful realities. HRM must understand these because they affect team dynamics and performance.

Common Defense Mechanisms and Workplace Examples:

Denial — Refusing to accept a reality. Example: An engineer receives poor performance feedback from their manager but insists "the evaluation system is broken" rather than acknowledging their own shortcomings. At DPDC, a technician who consistently makes wiring errors might deny that his work is at fault when circuit failures are traced back to him.

Projection — Attributing your own feelings or faults to others. Example: An engineer who is actually behind on their deliverable starts blaming the testing team for "slowing everything down." In reality, their own code is buggy, but they project the fault outward.

Rationalization — Creating logical-sounding justifications for unacceptable behavior. Example: An engineer who arrives 45 minutes late to a critical morning meeting explains that "traffic in Dhaka is impossible at this hour" — every day. The real reason is poor time management, but a plausible explanation is constructed to justify it.

Regression — Reverting to immature or childish behavior under stress. Example: During a high-pressure project deadline, an otherwise professional engineer starts throwing things, crying, or having emotional outbursts. The pressure causes regression to less mature coping behaviors.

Displacement — Redirecting emotions from the actual source to a safer target. Example: A project manager gets criticized by the CEO in a meeting. Instead of addressing the issue with the CEO, he comes back to the office and aggressively criticizes his junior engineers for minor errors. The anger is displaced onto a safer target.

Role of HR in Managing Defense Mechanisms:

Good HRM creates an environment where these mechanisms are less necessary by:

  • Providing employee counseling — someone to talk to when stress is high.
  • Running stress management training — teaching healthier coping strategies.
  • Developing Emotional Intelligence (EI) — helping managers and engineers recognize and manage their own emotions.
  • Building a positive workplace culture — where feedback is constructive, not threatening, so employees don't need to defend themselves as much.

10. Productivity and Job Satisfaction

What is Productivity?

Productivity = Output / Input

In an EEE context: if a team of 5 engineers (input) completes the design of a 33kV distribution network (output) in 10 days, their productivity is measurable. If another team of 5 takes 25 days for the same task, they are less productive.

Factors Affecting Employee Productivity:

  • Skills and training (an engineer trained in AutoCAD completes designs faster).
  • Work environment (a clean, safe, well-lit workshop improves output).
  • Motivation level (a motivated engineer finds better solutions faster).
  • Leadership quality (a good team lead removes obstacles and keeps the team focused).
  • Technology and tools availability (an engineer using MATLAB simulation tools is far faster than one doing everything manually).

What is Job Satisfaction?

Job satisfaction is the level of happiness or contentment an employee feels about their job. It is an emotional and psychological state that reflects how well the job meets a person's expectations and needs.

Key Point from the slide: Satisfied employees show less turnover and less absenteeism. An engineer who loves their job at Walton's R&D division doesn't call in sick unnecessarily and doesn't send their CV to competitors.

Factors Affecting Job Satisfaction:

  • Salary and benefits (fair pay relative to market).
  • Job security (stable employment contract, especially important in Bangladesh's private sector).
  • Work-life balance (not forcing 80-hour weeks every week).
  • Career growth opportunity (clear promotion path, training programs).
  • Workplace culture (respectful colleagues and supervisors).

Relationship Between Productivity and Satisfaction

These two are interdependent, not independent:

  • Satisfied employees → More productive (they care about doing good work).
  • High productivity → Recognition and success → Increased satisfaction (achievement feeds back into happiness).
  • Poor satisfaction → Low productivity → Poor results → More stress → Even lower satisfaction. (A vicious cycle.)

Real-World Example: Grameenphone consistently appears on "best employer" lists in Bangladesh. Their engineers report high job satisfaction. This directly correlates with the fact that GP's network uptime and service quality are consistently better than state-owned Teletalk's — whose employees report far lower job satisfaction due to poor pay, limited growth, and bureaucratic frustration.

Three Key Concepts from the Slide (for Exam):

Job Satisfaction: The degree to which an individual feels positively or negatively about various aspects of a job.

Job Involvement: The extent to which an individual is dedicated to a job. A highly involved engineer doesn't just "do the job" — they think about it, invest in it, and take pride in it. High involvement leads to higher quality output.

Job Performance: The quantity and quality of tasks accomplished by an individual or group at work. This is the measurable output — number of designs completed, error rate in installations, speed of fault resolution, etc.

11. Job Analysis

What is Job Analysis?

Job analysis is a formal, scientific, and systematic study of a specific job — collecting all relevant facts about what the job requires, what the person doing it actually does, and what skills/conditions are needed.

It answers: What does this job actually involve? What skills does the person need? What are the working conditions?

Job analysis produces two key documents:

  • Job Description — What the job entails (tasks, responsibilities, tools used, working conditions).
  • Job Specification — What the person must bring (qualifications, skills, experience, physical requirements).

Real-World Example: Before PGCB hires a Protection Engineer, they conduct a job analysis to determine: what relay testing the person will do, what voltage levels they'll work with, what software (e.g., DIGSI for Siemens relays) they need to know, and what physical conditions (outdoor substations, travel to remote sites) the role involves. This job analysis forms the basis of the job description advertised and the selection criteria used in interviews.

Objectives of Job Analysis (8 Uses — All Exam-Important):

1. Job Redesign — Understanding a job fully allows management to restructure it for better efficiency or ergonomics.

2. Work Standards — Defining how long a task should take and what quality is acceptable (e.g., a standard for how many transformer installations a team can complete per week).

3. Recruitment — Knowing exactly what a job requires allows HR to advertise it accurately and attract the right candidates.

4. Selection — Job analysis provides the criteria for choosing between candidates (what questions to ask, what skills to test).

5. Training — Identifying skill gaps between what the job requires and what new hires bring allows HR to design targeted training programs.

6. Performance Appraisal — You can only evaluate performance fairly if you first clearly define what "good performance" looks like for that specific job.

7. Job Evaluation — Comparing the relative worth of different jobs within the organization to ensure pay equity (e.g., ensuring a Senior Protection Engineer is paid more than a Metering Technician based on job complexity and responsibility).

8. Safety — Identifying physical risks in a job (high-voltage exposure, confined spaces, chemical handling) so that appropriate safety protocols and PPE requirements can be specified.

12. Personnel Development — Hiring and Wage Systems

What is Personnel Development?

Personnel development is the ongoing process of improving employee skills, knowledge, and capabilities so that both the employee and the organization grow together. It includes:

  • Hiring (bringing in the right people)
  • Training (building skills)
  • Career Development (growing within the organization)

Without personnel development, an organization hires people, extracts their current skills, and then those employees either stagnate or leave. With it, the organization continuously builds internal capability — creating future leaders and specialists from within.

Real-World Example: Intel's "rotation program" for new engineering graduates places them in multiple departments (design, fabrication, testing, quality) over their first two years. This is personnel development — deliberately growing their skills and understanding of the full operation before placing them in a permanent role.

13. Hiring in HRM

Hiring is the process of attracting, selecting, and onboarding qualified candidates for open positions. Doing it well is critical because a wrong hire is expensive — you pay them, train them, and then either suffer their underperformance or go through the costly process of letting them go and starting over.

Steps in the Hiring Process:

Step 1 — Manpower Planning Before hiring, determine: How many people do we need? In which roles? When? This prevents both overstaffing (wasting salary budget) and understaffing (overloading existing employees).

Step 2 — Job Analysis and Job Description (Covered in Section 11.) Define exactly what the job is and what the person needs.

Step 3 — Recruitment (Internal / External) Generating a pool of candidates.

  • Internal Recruitment: Promotion from within (a Junior Engineer becomes a Senior Engineer), or transfer between departments. Cheaper, faster, boosts morale. But limits fresh ideas.
  • External Recruitment: Campus recruitment at BUET or SUST, job portals (bdjobs.com, LinkedIn), recruitment agencies, employee referrals. Brings new skills and perspectives.

Step 4 — Selection and Interview Screening CVs, written tests (technical knowledge), and interviews (technical + behavioral). For EEE roles: companies like Bashundhara Group or Summit Power test candidates on power systems, circuit theory, and practical problem-solving.

Step 5 — Job Offer and Onboarding Making the formal offer, and then helping the new hire integrate into the organization — introducing them to the team, explaining systems and processes, and ensuring they have the tools and access they need.

Hiring Challenges in the Engineering Sector:

  • Skill gap in fresh graduates: Many EEE graduates from mid-tier universities lack practical industrial skills (hands-on experience with protection relays, SCADA systems, etc.).
  • High competition for skilled engineers: Top engineers from BUET or SUST receive multiple offers simultaneously.
  • Technology skill mismatch: Companies upgrading to smart grid or Industry 4.0 technologies find that available candidates were trained on older systems.
  • Retention of trained employees: After investing in training a fresh hire for 1–2 years, if the salary package isn't competitive, the now-skilled engineer leaves for a better offer.

14. Wage Systems

Objectives of a Wage System:

A wage system is not just about paying people — it is a strategic tool with specific objectives:

  • Attract talented employees (competitive pay brings skilled candidates to your organization).
  • Motivate employees to perform better (performance-linked pay incentivizes effort).
  • Ensure internal equity (similar jobs paid similarly within the organization) and external equity (pay is competitive with the market).
  • Support organizational goals (align individual financial incentives with company targets).

Types of Wage Systems:

1. Time Rate Wage System Payment is based purely on time worked — hourly, daily, weekly, or monthly.

  • Simple to understand and administer.
  • No direct incentive to work faster or better.
  • Suitable for jobs where output quality matters more than speed, or where output is difficult to measure.
  • Example: A salaried Protection Engineer at PGCB gets paid monthly regardless of how many relay tests they complete. The job requires precision, not speed.

2. Piece Rate Wage System Payment is based on the number of units produced.

  • Direct incentive to produce more.
  • Risk: employees may sacrifice quality for quantity.
  • Suitable for manufacturing/assembly work where output is easily counted.
  • Example: A subcontractor wiring technician who is paid per meter of cable installed in a factory.

3. Incentive Wage System A combination — base salary (like time rate) plus bonuses based on performance or output above a standard.

  • Balances stability (base salary) with motivation (bonus for extra output).
  • Example: A Grameenphone field engineer who has a fixed monthly salary but receives a quarterly bonus if their assigned tower uptime exceeds 99.5%.

4. Performance-Based Pay Pay is directly linked to individual, team, or organizational performance metrics — evaluated through the performance appraisal system.

  • Common in tech and managerial roles.
  • Example: Google's "SWE performance review" determines a significant portion of an engineer's annual compensation through a performance rating.

Factors Affecting Wage System Design:

  • Job Complexity — More complex jobs (designing a 132kV substation protection scheme) deserve higher pay than simpler ones (reading digital meters).
  • Skill Level Requirement — Rare skills command higher wages (e.g., HVDC system engineers).
  • Market Wage Rate — If the market pays ৳80,000/month for a power systems engineer, paying ৳45,000 won't attract good candidates.
  • Company Financial Condition — A startup can't pay MNC-level salaries regardless of intent.
  • Government Labor Policy — Minimum wage laws, mandatory overtime pay, provident fund requirements all shape what a company must pay.

15. Factors Affecting HR (External Environment)

HR managers don't operate in a vacuum. External forces constantly change the challenges and opportunities they face:

  • Legislation — Labor laws in Bangladesh (Bangladesh Labor Act 2006) set minimum standards for wages, working hours, leave, and termination. HR must comply or face legal penalties.
  • Economic Environment — During recession, companies freeze hiring. During growth periods, competition for talent intensifies. Bangladesh's RMG sector boom, for example, created massive HR pressure as factories competed for the same pool of skilled workers.
  • Global Economy — When global oil prices drop, power plant investment slows, and demand for power engineers decreases. When global tech investment is high (as in 2020–2021), Bangladeshi software engineers were being recruited heavily by remote-work opportunities abroad.
  • Structure of the Labor Market — Is there a surplus of engineers (making hiring easy but retention less critical) or a shortage (making both hiring and retention critical)? In Bangladesh, there is a surplus of civil engineers but a relative shortage of highly skilled power electronics specialists.
  • Political Environment — Political instability can freeze foreign investment, reduce project pipelines, and stall government infrastructure projects — directly reducing demand for engineering HR.
  • Changes in Technology — When SCADA systems replaced manual substation control, operators needed retraining. When 5G rollout began, telecom companies needed engineers with RF network skills. Technology changes continuously create skill gaps that HR must address through training or new hiring.
  • Need to Constantly Retrain Staff — Technology changes so fast in the engineering sector that a skill set can become outdated within 5–7 years. A protection engineer who learned electromechanical relay settings in 2005 needs retraining for numerical relay systems today. HR must budget for and manage ongoing retraining programs.
